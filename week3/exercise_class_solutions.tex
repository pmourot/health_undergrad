\textbf{Exercise 1:} Understanding a proposed health insurance plan

Plan A with the following parameters:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Premium: \$1,200 per year
    \item Deductible: \$1,000
    \item Coinsurance: 10\%
    \item Out-of-pocket max: \$2,500
\end{itemize}
\vspace{3mm}

\textbf{Questions}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[1.] Draw the ralationship between out-of-pocket costs (y-axis) and total health care expenses (x-axis) for this plan. \textit{Note: do not include the premium in this graph.}

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}

    \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{input/exercise1_graph.pdf}
    \end{center}    


    How to get $X$?
    \begin{align*}
        1,000 + 0.1(X - 1,000) &= 2,500 \\
        X - 1,000 &= \frac{1500}{0.1} \\
        X &= 16,000
    \end{align*}
    }

    \item[2.] Someone who has this plan breaks their arm and generate \$400 in medical bills. That's their only contact with the medical system that year. How much do they pay out of pocket for this encounter?

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}

    They pay \$400 because they are in the deductible region.
    }

    \item[3.] This person is later told that they need an MRI to assess whether there is any damage to ther ligaments. The MRI generates another \$3,000 in medical bills. 
    This happens the same year as the broken arm. How much do they pay out of pocket for the MRI (not including that broken arm bill, which was separate)?

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}

    First, they pay the rest of the deductible: $\$1,000 - \$400 = \$600$.
    Then, they pay 10\% coinsurance on the remaining amount:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Remaining amount of MRI bill: \$3,000 - 600 = \$2,400
        \item Coinsurance: 10\% of \$2,400 = \$240
    \end{itemize}
    In total, they pay \$600 + \$240 = \$840 for the MRI.
    }

    \item[4.] Another person on this plan takes Capaxone each month to treat their multiple sclerosis. The drug costs \$1,000 per month.
    In addition, this person anticipates \$8,000 in various medical costs this same year.
    How much should they anticipate to spend out of pocket this year when enrolled in this plan?
    If things get much worse than anticipated for this person,
    should they worry that their out of pocket costs would be higher?


    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}

    This person anticipates to reach the maximum out of pocket cost, so they expect to pay \$2,500 out of pocket this year.
    If things get worse, they should not worry about higher out of pocket costs because they have already reached the out-of-pocket maximum.
    }

\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Exercise 2:} Comparing two plans when minimizing own healthcare spending

You are trying to compare two health insurance plans and you want to know which one would minimize what you would have to pay in healthcare this year.
Two plans are proposed to you:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Plan A: premium \$1,200 per year, deductible \$1,000, coinsurance 10\%, out-of-pocket max \$2,500
    \item Plan B: premium \$0 per year, deductible \$2,000, coinsurance 14.3\%, out-of-pocket max \$4,000
\end{enumerate}
\vspace{3mm}

\textbf{Questions}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item[1.] Draw the relationship between total consumer-paid expenses (premium + out-of-pocket costs) (y-axis) and total health care expenses (x-axis) for these two plans.

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}

    \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{input/exercise2_graph.pdf}
    \end{center}    

    How to get $X^A$ and $X^B$?\\
    Lets start with $X^B$ that corresponds to plan B:
    \begin{align*}
        2,000 + (X^B - 2,000) \times 0.143 &= 4,000\\
        X^B &= (4,000 - 2,000) \times \frac{1}{0.143} + 2,000\\
        X^B &\approx 15,986.014
    \end{align*}

    What about $X^A$ for plan B? There are two correct ways to do it.
    Either
    \begin{align*}
        2,200 + (X^A - 1,000) \times 0.1 &= 3,700\\
        X^A = (3,700-2,200) \times \frac{1}{0.1} + 1,000\\
        X^A = 16,000
    \end{align*}
    or you can forget about the premium and use the following
    \begin{align*}
        1,000 + (X^A - 1,000) \times 0.1 &= 2,500\\
        X^A = (2,500-1,000) \times \frac{1}{0.1} + 1,000\\
        X^A = 16,000
    \end{align*}
    Both are correct and give the same answer.
    This is because including the premium only implies a \textit{translation up} of the curve, but changes nothing else.

    Note that $X^A$ and $X^B$ are extremely close to each other; they are almost equal.
    }


    \item[2.] Which plan minimizes total consumer-paid expenses for someone who expects to have \$3,000 in total health care expenses this year?

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}

    Plan B.
    }


    \item[3.] Which plan minimizes total consumer-paid expenses for someone who expects to have \$17,000 in total health care expenses this year?

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}

    Plan A.
    }

    \item[4.] At what level of total healthcare spending does plan A becomes better than plan B in terms of minimizing total consumer-paid expenses?

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}

    \begin{center}
        \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{input/exercise2b_graph.pdf}
    \end{center}  

    In the graph, it looks like it happens in the coinsurance region.
    The exact point is at the intersection of the two lines in the coinsurance region $I$.
    You need to find the equation for the two lines, and then solve for their intersection.

    For plan A, this is an affine function of the form $y = a \times x + b$ (since it is linear) where
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $a = 0.1$ (coinsurance rate), since we are in the coinsurance region.
        \item We need to determine the intercept $b$.
        To do that, take a point in the coinsurance region, and solve for $b$.
        Let's take the point $(1,000,2,200)$.
        \begin{align*}
            2,200 &= 0.1 \times 1,000 + b \\
            b &= 2,200 - 100 \\
            b &= 2,100
        \end{align*}
    \end{itemize}
    The line for plan A is $y = 0.1 \times x + 2,100$.

    For plan B, this is an affine function of the form $y = c \times x + d$ where
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $c = 0.143$ (coinsurance rate), since we are in the coinsurance region.
        \item We need to determine the intercept $d$.
        To do that, take a point in the coinsurance region, and solve for $d$.
        Let's take the point $(2,000,2,000)$.
        \begin{align*}
            2,000 &= 0.143 \times 2,000 + d \\
            d &= 2,000 - 286 \\
            d &= 1,714
        \end{align*}
    \end{itemize}
    The line for plan B is $y = 0.143 \times x + 1,714$.

    Now, what is the point $I$ where the two lines intersect?
    \begin{align*}
        0.1 \times I + 2,100 &= 0.143 \times I + 1,714 \\
        2,100 - 1,714 &= 0.143 \times I - 0.1 \times I \\
        386 &= 0.043 \times I \\
        I &= \frac{386}{0.043} \\
        I &\approx 8,976.74
    \end{align*}

    Therefore, plan A becomes better than plan B for total healthcare spending above approximately \$8,976.74.

    }

\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Exercise 3:} Buying health insurance

The consumer has \$20,000 in income and her utility function is $u(x) = \sqrt(x)$.\\
She faces health risks such that
\begin{itemize}
    \item[-] She has a 50\% chance of having no medical expenses
    \item[-] She has a 40\% chance of having \$1,000 in medical expenses (mild illness)
    \item[-] She has a 10\% chance of having \$10,000 in medical expenses (severe illness)
\end{itemize}
\vspace{3mm}

She is considering buying a health insurance plan with
\begin{itemize}
    \item[-] A premium of \$1,000
    \item[-] A deductible of \$500
    \item[-] A coinsurance rate of 20\%
\end{itemize}

{\color{red} Note that the two previous exercises were asking you to compare plans in terms of consumer's expenses.
However, consumers may value other things than only minimizing their expenses,
such as avoiding a risky gamble, which would factor in utility and risk-preferences.
This third exercise now asks you about what consumers would \textit{prefer} if they wanted to maximize their utility
(note that you are now given a utility function),
rather than only minimizing their expected own healthcare expenses.
}

\vspace{3mm}

\textbf{Questions}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item What is the consumer's expected out-of-pocket cost when uninsured?

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}

    The expected out-of-pocket cost is
    \begin{align*}
    \mathbb{E}(\text{OOP})
    &= 0.5 \times \$0 + 0.4 \times \$1{,}000 + 0.1 \times \$10,000\\
    &= \$1{,}400.
    \end{align*}
    The expected out-of-pocket cost when the individual is uninsured is \$1,400.
    }

    \item What is the expected cost to the insurer?

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}

    The expected cost to the insurer is
    \begin{align*}
    \mathbb{E}(\text{Cost}_{insurer})
    &= 0.5 \times \$0 + 0.4 \times \left( (\$1{,}000 - \$500) \times 0.8 \right) + 0.1 \times \left( (\$10{,}000 - \$500) \times 0.8 \right) \\
    &= \$160 + \$760 \\
    &= \$920.
    \end{align*}
    The expected cost to the insurer is \$920.
    }

    \item What is the expected profit to the insurer?

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}
    The expected profit to the insurer is
    \begin{align*}
    \mathbb{E}(\text{Profit}_{insurer})
    &= \text{Premium} - \mathbb{E}(\text{Cost}_{insurer}) \\
    &= \$1{,}000 - \$920 \\
    &= \$80.
    \end{align*}
    The expected profit to the insurer is \$80.
    }

    \item What would be the actuarially fair price for this insurance plan?
    \textit{Hint: the actuarially fair price is the premium that would give the insurer zero expected profit.}\\

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}
    The actuarially fair price for this insurance plan is equal to the expected cost to the insurer,
    which we calculated in question 2:
    \begin{align*}
    \text{Actuarially fair price} = \mathbb{E}(\text{Cost}_{insurer}) = \$920.
    \end{align*}
    }

    \item Does the consumer prefer to remain uninsured or to buy this insurance plan?

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}
    To determine the consumer's preference, we need to calculate the expected utility in both scenarios.
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Uninsured:
        \begin{align*}
        \mathbb{E}(U_{uninsured}) &= 0.5 \times \sqrt{20{,}000} + 0.4 \times \sqrt{20{,}000 - 1,000} + 0.1 \times \sqrt{20{,}000 - 10,000} \\
        &\approx 135.8469.
        \end{align*}

        \item Insured:
        \begin{align*}
        \mathbb{E}(U_{insured}) &= 0.5 \times \sqrt{20{,}000 - \underbrace{1,000}_{\text{premium}}} \\
        & + 0.4 \times \sqrt{20,000 - 500 - (1,000-500) \times 0.2 - \underbrace{1,000}_{\text{premium}}} \\
        &+ 0.1 \times \sqrt{20{,}000 - 500 - (10,000-500) \times 0,2 - \underbrace{1,000}_{\text{premium}}} \\
        &\approx 136.06.
        \end{align*}
    \end{itemize}
    Since $\mathbb{E}(U_{insured}) > \mathbb{E}(U_{uninsured})$, the consumer prefers to buy this insurance plan.
    }

    \item What is the maximum premium the consumer would be willing to pay for this insurance plan?

    \vspace{3mm}
    { \color{darkblue}
    Let $P_{max}$ be the maximum premium the consumer is willing to pay.
    We need to solve for $P_{max}$ such that the expected utility when insured
    equals the expected utility when uninsured:
    \begin{align*}
    & \mathbb{E}(U_{insured, P_{max}}) = \mathbb{E}(U_{uninsured})\\
    \leftrightarrow &0.5 \times \sqrt{20{,} - P_{max}} + 0.4 \times \sqrt{20,000 - 500 - (1000-500) \times 0.2 - P_{max}} \\
    &\text{  }+ 0.1 \times \sqrt{20{,}000 - 500 - (10,000-500) \times 0,2 - P_{max}} \approx 135.8469\\
    \end{align*}

    You can solve this equation numerically using Wolfram alpha (\url{https://www.wolframalpha.com/input}) or statistical softwares like R.
    Solving this equation numerically, we find that $P_{max} \approx 1,058.74$.
    }

\end{enumerate}
